Independent Science Project: Roundup's Effect on Human Gut Bacteria

Abstract:

Roundup is an herbicide commonly used by the agricultural industry to kill weeds. Roundup residual can be found on agricultural products. It cannot be washed off and it is not destroyed by cooking. Glyphosate, the active ingredient Roundup, has been found in human body fluids, such as urine and blood. It has also been shown to adversely affect gut bacteria in animals. However, glyphosateâ€™s effect on the human gut microbiome is unknown. Gut dysbiosis, an imbalance in the beneficial and detrimental bacteria in the human gut, has been associated with many diseases such as cancer, diabetes, and depression. This project evaluated if Roundup adversely affected beneficial human gut bacteria more than harmful bacteria. Models of beneficial (C. Sporegenes) and detrimental (E. Coli) bacteria were treated with serial dilutions of Roundup (1% down to 0.0001%). Water was used as the control. Bacterial growth was measured using a colorimeter set to Optical Density (OD) 565. Labquest was used to measure the OD every 10 minutes. GraphPad Prism v6.0 was used to analyze the data after normalizing maximal absorbance to controls. The dose at which 50% of C. Sporogenes was inhibited was more than an order of magnitude smaller than that of E. Coli (5.538E-4 vs 9.086E-3, P<0.0001). These data imply that human exposure to Roundup residue on food products can cause gut dysbiosis. This may lead to serious disease in people. More studies are needed to see if avoiding foods with Roundup residue should be recommended.
